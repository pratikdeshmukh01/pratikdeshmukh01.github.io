The Formula Behind Billions of Dollars in Equity Valuation

Why Analysts Use It. Why Critics Attack It.
═══════════════════════════════
Research: Pratik Deshmukh | 10 Minute Read

Valuation is often described as a mix of art and science. But what happens when the "science" relies on an assumption that is mathematically constrained in the real world?

At first glance, the Gordon Growth Model (GGM) seems overly simplistic. Walk into any investment bank or valuation boutique, however, and you will find this formula deeply embedded in their financial models. Let's break down the mechanics, the constraints, and the true utility of this foundational model.

Deconstructing Growth: The DuPont Reality Check

The GGM relies on a fundamental accounting identity: g = Retention Ratio × ROE. To understand why a perpetual constant growth rate is virtually impossible, we must visually unbundle ROE.

Most finance textbooks assume: g = Retention × ROE.

However, DuPont Analysis visually proves that ROE itself depends on three distinct, constantly shifting operational and financial variables.

Therefore:

Constant growth is not a natural law. It is an assumption.

⚠ Challenge the Model
If ROE depends on:
• Profit Margin
• Asset Turnover
• Leverage

And these variables constantly change due to business cycles...

Can growth truly remain constant forever?
You are correct. The strict assumptions are unrealistic, making GGM a terminal-state proxy, not a real-time predictor.

The Danger of Sensitivity

Because the valuation denominator is (r - g), the model is violently sensitive to small changes. Look at what happens to intrinsic value (assuming a ₹10 next-year dividend and a 10% required return) as growth shifts.

Growth Rate (g) Intrinsic Value (P₀)
4%₹ 166
5%₹ 200
6%₹ 250
7%₹ 333
Key Takeaway: A mere 1% change in your growth assumption (from 5% to 6%) increases the entire valuation by 25%.

Real World Application: Where it Works and Fails

Why GGM Works

Coca-Cola | Johnson & Johnson | P&G
  • Consistent dividend payer
  • Highly stable profit margins
  • Mature business cycle
  • Growth rate mirrors broad GDP inflation

Why GGM Fails

Tesla (2015) | Amazon (2005) | Nvidia (Early)
  • No stable dividends
  • Explosive, unpredictable growth
  • Constantly shifting ROE due to heavy reinvestment
  • Formula breaks entirely if g > r

The Real Question

The debate is not whether the Gordon Growth Model is wrong.

The debate is whether investors understand the assumptions
they are making.


Every valuation model is wrong.

Some are useful.


The Gordon Growth Model remains
one of the most useful.
